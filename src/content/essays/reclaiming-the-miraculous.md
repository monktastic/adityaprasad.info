> _Reality is that which, when you stop believing in it, doesn't go away._ —Philip K Dick

It is not possible to be certain about the nature of reality—to know what's "really happening." But you can be sure that something **seems** to be happening. It's what we call "experience."

If you can hold both of these at once with precision—which people almost never do—something remarkable and unexpected happens. The profound ennui and meaninglessness devouring our civilization: their source becomes obvious. And so does the way out.

Our world is not one of "pitiless indifference," as we've been telling ourselves. It is one of **pure, miraculous magic.**

Far from being irrational, it's inevitable when reason is taken to its logical conclusion.

---

Look around at this remarkable experience called "life." Take a moment to really soak it in.

What is it? What's causing it?

The answer seems obvious: there's a physical world, you experience it through your brain, etc.

But do you really know that? How do you know it's not all a simulation, or a dream, etc.? Even if you can't know for *sure*, don't such possibilities feel unlikely?

But how could you ever calculate those odds? You would have to resort to assumptions about whatever is *outside* this reality—assumptions which are, by definition, untestable. So the feeling that it's "unlikely" is just that: a *feeling*.

Can you even be sure there *was* a reality before five minutes ago? Maybe it all spontaneously popped into being, fully formed, at that moment, with only the *appearance* of a real past (including planted evidence, false memories, etc.). How can you evaluate the probability without trusting knowledge from *before* five minutes ago—which would be circular?

Even Occam's Razor cannot help you here, relying as it does on "past" evidence.

In fact, for all you know, reality is magically popping into existence right now. When? Now. Yes, **now**. There *was* no "two seconds ago." You're imagining it.

Sure, maybe this possibility is no more likely than any other. But it's not any *less*, either. Again, it may *feel* profoundly unlikely—but only if you assume your "past knowledge" is true.

Just let yourself recognize: *I genuinely do not—**cannot**—know what this is.*

Maybe you think: "oh, it doesn't matter what's 'actually' true. I'm just acting *as though* the usual view were true, for practical purposes." But is that really it? Or is there a part of you that feels a visceral sense of it being true, *prior* to any thoughts about it? **That's** what we're looking to disarm.

Take your time ("time") with this, until it really starts to sink in. Even if you've done this before, do it again now. Don't just imagine or recall what it's like.

If done carefully, you'll feel the ground wobbling beneath your feet. A kind of giddy, almost uncanny feeling will begin to overtake you. It's not something new or foreign. It's just closer to what things are like *before* we put conceptual overlays on them.

It can help to notice: we're not doing anything strange or mystical here. We're just being as radically honest as humanly possible.

Even if you catch a taste, your beliefs will start to powerfully reassert themselves—even though you can prove they are unjustified. It feels like "oh come on, get real." Isn't that what happens? What's going on there? 

Something in us cannot tolerate us seriously questioning our reality. What would happen if you got underneath that part of yourself?

---

Although you cannot say precisely **what** is happening, it's undoubtable that **something** is happening, isn't it[^denial]? Or at least, that it **seems** to be?

Don't just think about this. Look at the vibrant, wondrous experience engulfing you. Really drink it in. All the sights, sounds, textures, etc.

If you begin to doubt that something really seems to be happening, simply notice how *doubt seems to be happening*. This is not an idea. Not a philosophical argument. Just *look*, as a child might. Don't label it—"consciousness" or whatever.

Don't proceed until you are left with **absolutely no doubt**. Until you feel the self-defeating nature of doubting **this**.

So on one hand, you're engulfed in something **completely undoubtable**. On the other, any "explanation" for it is utterly meaningless. Unjustifiable. You don't even have reason to believe you've experienced anything before this very moment.

Which moment? **This** one. Which one? This one. Etc.

It helps to remind yourself that you're not doing anything weird. You're simply doing the most honest thing possible: remaining with what you can be **completely sure** of, while releasing all unjustified (and unjustifiable) beliefs about what it "is" or what's "causing" it.

If you manage to rest in this apparent paradox—nailing both halves with perfect precision—a creeping recognition will begin to dawn.

You're in direct contact with something that is literally beyond explanation. Not in some "woo-woo" or hand-wavy sense. *Provably.* Inescapably. And yet, here it is—radically, almost magically alive.

---

> _I wish I could show you when you are lonely or in darkness the astonishing light of your own being._ — Hafiz

Here's what happens when I do this: I begin to fear I'm being irrational or superstitious. How can something be genuinely mysterious? That just means that we haven't found an explanation *yet*. Surely science will eventually solve it (if it hasn't already). My (very real) brain is just having a trippy experience, that's all.

But **why** do I think that, when I've just proven this explanation is no more "likely" than any other? Doesn't it mean I've abandoned the profound honesty I was modeling just a moment ago? Why do I feel the need to do that?

Even if science *does* "solve" it, this exercise will still be available to me. In that case, why do I think of it as a "solution" at all?

There's also the fear that I will lose the ability to function. But is that true? I'm just learning to stop confusing the map with the territory. That's not the same as abandoning the map. And I cannot do it by simply *thinking harder about it*. That just gets me lost further in my own mind.

If I relax all these fears and persist, something astonishing happens. My experience undergoes a phase transition from mundane to **miraculous**. Or more precisely, I recognize that it has **always been this way, timelessly**.

Believe me, I know how the word "miraculous" sounds. I don't just mean "I can't explain it, therefore miracle." I mean I directly perceive the precise sense in which all explanations for it are "[not even wrong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Not_even_wrong)."

Some call this experience "God." It has the unmistakable flavor of "that than which nothing greater can be conceived.[^anselm]" Other people hate the G-word. Either way, I finally understand why people cannot help but use it.

You could also just call it *reality*: the one thing that does not "go away" when we stop believing in it.

---

Here's why I think we don't let ourselves go this deep.

What happens when you take something utterly for granted? When it's so obvious, so normal, that even bothering to question it feels absurd?

You **stop looking** at it.

And what happens when you stop looking at something? When you no longer really *see* it?

You feel fine treating it any which way you want.

And what does humanity as a whole seem to be doing to each other? To other beings? To nature?

We literally couldn't do it if we **saw** what we were looking at.

Don't take my word for it. Repeat this exercise while looking at another person, or animal, or plant. Doesn't it become **much harder** to use them for your own benefit while really seeing them?

And why do you really see them? Because you've stopped telling yourself stories *about* them and are instead looking directly *at* them. Again, it's not that you abandon provisional knowledge of how to interact. But a visceral layer of assumption disappears—and that changes everything.

Modeling this radical honesty: it would begin to reveal aspects of ourselves that we may not be ready to confront. And that might stop us from engaging in behavior we're not ready to give up.

---

Here's the amazing part: none of what I've just told you is controversial. Here's "poetic naturalist"[^carroll] (and respected physicist) Sean Carroll on the problem of skepticism:

> *There is* ***no way*** *to distinguish between the scenarios by collecting new data. [...] We have every right to give high credence to views of the world that are productive and fruitful, in preference to those that would leave us paralyzed with ennui.*

In other words: yes, there is no principled way to privilege any (internally consistent) model of reality over another. So why not take my personal favorite—the conventional view of time, space, matter, etc.—on faith?

See, life and consciousness are just accidental byproducts of mindless forces bashing lifeless matter against itself:

> *The universe that we observe has precisely the properties we should expect if there is, at bottom, no design, no purpose, no evil, no good, nothing but **pitiless indifference**.* —Richard Dawkins

Boy, good thing our civilization didn't adopt a perspective that would leave us "paralyzed with ennui," pillaging "fundamentally lifeless" nature for our own benefit.

It is no coincidence that the philosophy *assuming* reality is fundamentally lifeless shares its name with the social phenomenon rapidly *turning* our world lifeless: "materialism." When there's only one thing that "really exists," why *wouldn't* we spend our lives chasing after it? Sacrificing inert chunks of matter like "forests" and "ecosystems" in endless pursuit of it?

---

This, ultimately, is the choice on offer: to take on pure faith that we live in a world of "pitiless indifference," or to discover the precise sense in which it is radically, magically alive.

But no matter how hard I want to believe that reality is far greater than any boxes we've tried stuffing it in, the cultural milieu makes this exceptionally difficult.

In this environment, the part of me that *wants* to open up feels ridiculous doing so. Isn't it much more "sophisticated" to believe that all of this is nonsense? To get on with the noble effort of turning nature into goods and services, like our selfish genes demand?

Any suspicion to the contrary gets re-categorized as wishful thinking; a fantasy born of rogue neurotransmitters, ruthlessly evolved to distract us from the "cold, hard truth."

Which means that even if I catch glimpses of what's *actually* true, they're slower to transform me. I don't dare share them in places that aren't "safe," making them harder to integrate.

But that appears to be turning around in recent years. People are suspecting something is "off" about the way we're living; about the very ways we conceive of the world.

Which suggests that the time is finally right to rehabilitate "God" or "the miracle"—or if you prefer, the unspeakable magic of *life itself.*

It couldn't remain hidden forever.

> _When I've asked shamans about the sickness of the west, they say: "it's quite simple: you have severed your connection with spirit… unless you reconnect with spirit and do so soon, you're going to bring the whole house of cards down around your heads and ours."_
> 
> — Graham Hancock
